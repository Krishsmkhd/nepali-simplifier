Why Nepali Spelling Needs to
Catch Up With Nepali Speech

Our writing system carries centuries of Sanskrit inheritance — sounds we no longer make, distinctions we no longer draw. It is time to talk about a simpler, more honest orthography.

Krish Simkhada · March 2026 · Linguistics

When a Nepali child sits down to learn to write, they face a puzzle that has nothing to do with how they actually speak. They must memorise whether a word uses इ or ई, उ or ऊ, स, श, or ष. They will spend hours drilling these distinctions. And when they grow up, they will still make these "errors" in text messages — because something in them knows, even if they cannot articulate it, that the distinctions do not reflect how they actually talk.

This gap between spoken Nepali and written Nepali is not a sign of ignorance. It is a sign that our writing system has not kept pace with our language. Languages evolve. Spoken Nepali has evolved. The orthography, rooted in Sanskrit conventions formalised centuries ago, largely has not.

This essay is a proposal — not a decree — to think seriously about what a phonetically honest, practically simpler Nepali writing system might look like. And to propose alongside it a formal romanization system that works without special characters or workarounds.

The Sounds We Do Not Actually Make

Sanskrit distinguished three sibilant consonants: the dental स, the palatal श, and the retroflex ष. These are genuinely distinct sounds in classical Sanskrit. But in modern spoken Nepali, nearly all speakers — regardless of region, education, or dialect — produce a single sibilant sound. The three letters represent one phoneme.

Consider what this means in practice. The words संस्कृति, विश्वास, and देश each use a different sibilant letter, but a native speaker pronouncing all three uses the same sound in each. The three-way distinction exists only on the page.

"A writing system that requires distinctions the spoken language no longer makes is not preserving the language — it is preserving the fossil of an older one."

The same is true of the short and long vowels — what grammarians call ह्रस्व and दीर्घ. Written Nepali distinguishes इ from ई, and उ from ऊ. Spoken Nepali, for the overwhelming majority of speakers, does not. Knowing whether नेपाली takes the long ई or the short इ is a pure spelling convention, not a phonological fact.

And then there is ज्ञ, traditionally listed in the Nepali alphabet as a conjunct consonant pronounced something like the Sanskrit jña. Every Nepali speaker pronounces this as gya — the word ज्ञान is said gyaan. The spelling simply does not reflect the sound.

What the Reform Proposes

These observations lead naturally to a set of specific, principled changes. The key is that every change proposed here removes a distinction that no longer exists in speech — nothing is merged that speakers still distinguish. This is the guiding rule: the writing system should reflect the spoken language, not an idealised ancestor of it.

TraditionalReformedRomanReason
ईइ mergediLong/short vowel distinction not maintained in speech
ऊउ mergeduLong/short vowel distinction not maintained in speech
श / षस mergedsAll three sibilants pronounced identically
णन mergednRetroflex nasal indistinguishable from dental in speech
वब mergedbLabial approximant merges with ब in natural speech
ज्ञग्य reformedgyaActual Nepali pronunciation is gya, not Sanskrit jña
क्षक्स reformedkshaष merges into स; conjunct follows naturally
ङ, ञङ, ञ limitedng, nyRetained as conjunct nasals only — e.g. अङ्क, पञ्च

Crucially, the reform does not touch the retroflex consonants ट ठ ड ढ. These are genuinely distinct from their dental counterparts त थ द ध in spoken Nepali, and merging them would destroy real meaning. The words टाल and ताल mean different things. The distinction is alive and must be preserved.

The Devanagari script itself stays. This is not a proposal to romanize Nepali or to abandon the script — the script is part of our identity and is entirely capable of representing the reformed system.

The Paragraph, Before and After

Abstract principles are easier to grasp when you can see them in action. Here is a short paragraph in traditional Nepali, followed by the reformed orthography, and then the romanized form.

Traditional Nepali
नेपाल एउटा सुन्दर देश हो। नेपालीहरू आफ्नो संस्कृति र परम्परामा विश्वास गर्छन्। यहाँका मानिसहरू साहसी र परिश्रमी छन्।
Reformed Nepali
नेपाल एउटा सुन्दर देस हो। नेपालिहरु आफ्नो संस्क्रिति र परम्परामा बिस्बास गर्छन्। यहाँका मानिसहरु साहसि र परिस्रमि छन्।
Romanized — Reformed System
Nepal eut'a sundar des ho. Nepaliharu aaphno sanskriti ra parampara maa bisbaas garchhan. Yahaan ka maanisaharu saahasi ra parisrami chhan.

The reformed version is immediately readable to any Nepali speaker. Nothing is lost in meaning. What is gained is honesty — the text now reflects how people actually say these words.

A Formal Romanization — The Apostrophe System

Any serious proposal for a reformed Nepali writing system must also address romanization. Nepali speakers already write in Roman script constantly — in text messages, social media captions, and English-language documents with Nepali words. But there is no agreed standard. Everyone improvises, and the same word can be spelled a dozen different ways.

The system proposed here has one key design decision: retroflexes are marked with an apostrophe. So ट becomes t' and ठ becomes t'h. This solves two problems that capital-letter approaches cannot: it works in all-caps text (TAAL vs T'AAL is still distinct), and it works at the start of a sentence where a capital T would be ambiguous. All characters used exist on a standard keyboard.

The system also handles word boundaries thoughtfully. Word-final aa shortens to a — so एउटा is eut'a, not eut'aa. Word-final inherent a is dropped entirely — so देस is des, not desa. Postpositions and nipat — words like को, मा, ले — are exempt from these rules and keep their full vowels: ko, maa, le.

A Note on Proper Nouns

One deliberate exception in the romanization system concerns established international proper nouns. By the system's rules, नेपाल would consistently romanize as Nepaal — the पा carries an aa matra that is not word-final. However, Nepal has a globally recognised conventional English spelling used in diplomacy, journalism, and cartography. The same applies to Kathmandu, Pokhara, and similar place names. The proposed system treats these as proper noun exceptions, keeping their conventional spellings while applying the full ruleset to all other words.

What This Is Not

This proposal does not claim that the traditional script is wrong or that Sanskrit is irrelevant. The traditional orthography preserves etymological information — knowing that देश comes from a Sanskrit root that used श tells you something. This has real value in literary, religious, and scholarly contexts, and no one is proposing to abolish those forms.

It does not claim that spoken Nepali is uniform. Nepali is spoken across Nepal, India, Bhutan, and diaspora communities worldwide, and dialects vary. The observations here reflect the broad Central Nepali that underlies the written standard. And it does not claim to be final — this is a starting proposal, not a conclusion. The right outcome is sustained conversation among linguists, educators, publishers, and ordinary Nepali speakers.

Precedent: Other Languages Have Done This

Nepali would not be the first language to rationalize its writing system in response to phonological change. Turkish replaced its Arabic-derived script with Roman in 1928. German has undergone multiple spelling reforms in the twentieth century. Norwegian has had official spelling changes as recently as 2005. In each case the motivation was essentially the same: to bring writing closer to the way people actually speak and to reduce the burden on learners. The Nepali conversation is part of a wider pattern.

On the question of children: Perhaps the most practical argument for reform is what it would mean for primary education. The current system asks children to memorise distinctions that carry no phonological meaning — they cannot hear the difference between इ and ई because there is none in modern Nepali. A reformed system would significantly reduce this arbitrary memorisation burden, allowing more cognitive space for actual reading, writing, and comprehension.

Languages always evolve. The spoken form moves, and eventually — if we are thoughtful about it rather than passive — the written form can move with it. Nepali is a living language, spoken by tens of millions of people across the world. It deserves an orthography that reflects the living language, not just its classical inheritance.

I am not a professional linguist. I am a Nepali speaker who thinks about language. The ideas here are a starting point, not a conclusion. I would genuinely welcome responses — corrections, counterarguments, extensions, and improvements — from anyone who cares about where this language is going.
